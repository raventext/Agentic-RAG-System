Abstract
We introduce the METAGAME, a conceptual framework for quantifying second-
order interaction effects of model explanations. For any first-order attribution ϕ(f)
explaining a model f, we measure the directional influence of feature j on the attri-
bution of feature i, denoted as meta-attribution φj→i(f), by treating the attribution
method itself as a cooperative game and computing its Shapley value. Theoretically,
we prove that attributions hierarchically decompose into meta-attributions, and
establish these as directional extensions of existing interaction indices. Empirically,
we demonstrate that the METAGAME delivers insights across diverse interpretabil-
ity applications: (i) quantifying token interactions in instruction-tuned language
models, (ii) explaining cross-modal similarity in vision-language encoders, and
(iii) interpreting text-to-image concepts in multimodal diffusion transformers.
Introduction
Attribution is the task of quantifying how specific variables, such as input tokens, training data, or
internal components, influence a machine learning model’s behavior. Attribution methods serve as the
cornerstone of key interpretability approaches, driving advancements in concept-based explainability
for vision models (Fel et al., 2023), circuit discovery (Syed et al., 2024), data valuation (Wang
et al., 2025), and instruction-following in language models (Komorowski et al., 2026). Despite
their broad utility, simple attributions based on the notions of cooperative game theory (Lundberg
and Lee, 2017), gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017), or relevance propagation (Achtibat et al.,
2024) are limited by capturing only first-order influence of a single variable, omitting higher-order
dynamics encoded by the model, such as synergies and antisynergies (see an example in Figure 1).
To this end, interaction methods that measure the second-order influence of input pairs, such as
Shapley interactions (Fumagalli et al., 2023) and Integrated Hessians (Janizek et al., 2021), have
been proposed. However, it remains an open challenge whether, and how, an arbitrary first-order
attribution method can be generalized from first principles to capture higher-order interactions.
We answer this question by revisiting the concept of explaining explanations (Janizek et al., 2021),
abstracting their serial use of integrated gradients to quantify how a particular input feature influ-
ences an attribution value of another feature. Lundstrom and Razaviyayn (2023) have theoretically
studied the interaction leakage into individual effects of such serial methods. Furthermore, explicitly
computing continuous second-order derivatives like input Hessians requires architectural modifi-
cations (Dombrowski et al., 2019; Janizek et al., 2021) and computational tricks for large input
sizes (Pramanik et al., 2026). Moreover, the combinatorial growth of set-based interactions limits
their comprehensibility in practice. We thus propose a general meta-approach that circumvents these
limitations by decomposing any arbitrary first-order attribution into directional interaction terms.

Key contributions. Our work advances literature in multiple ways:
(1) We deliver a principled and effective approach to generalize any first-order attribution method to
quantify second-order interactions via the Shapley value. We name these methods meta-attributions.
(2) We propose a hierarchical decomposition of attributions via directional interactions. This perspec-
tive provides a practical way to navigate the combinatorial complexity of interactions. Surprisingly,
we show that Shapley interactions and integrated Hessians already implicitly do this (Theorem 1).
(3) We introduce the METAGAME and meta-attributions to define a novel directional interaction index,
and we prove this serves as a directional extension of existing interaction indices (Theorem 2).
(4) Finally, we demonstrate that METAGAME and meta-attributions offer pragmatic insights when
applied to interpret large language models, vision-language encoders, and diffusion transformers.
Theoretical Background
We consider a general feature/token attribution method ϕi(f) that quantifies the influence of feature
i ∈[d] := {1, . . . , d} on a predictor f : Rd →R. While broadly applicable, we predominantly
focus on local explanations at a fixed input x ∈Rd, denoted as ϕi(f, x). To evaluate f on subsets,
we define the masked predictor f(S; x), e.g. via baseline imputation f(S; x) := f(xS, b ¯S), which
retains features S ⊆[d] and replaces the remainder ¯S := [d] \ S with a baseline b ∈Rd.

2.2
Limitations of Existing Interaction Measures
Combinatorial explosion in set-based interactions. A major limitation of existing order-k interac-
tion indices is the resulting O(dk) terms, which become increasingly difficult to interpret as feature
dimensions or interaction orders grow. By collapsing interactions into undirected subsets {i, j},
these set-based approaches fail to capture the directional hierarchy necessary to explain how features
sequentially build upon one another, resulting in a redundant and less intuitive set of terms.
Limited flexibility and separation leakage. Faithfully separating pure individual effects from
joint interactions remains a non-trivial challenge. Serial methods inherently fail to do this, leaking
interaction terms into the individual diagonal components. While Shapley interactions and SOP
attempt to isolate these effects (see Table 1 for an example), Shapley interactions treat the model as a
black box, and SOP has been studied exclusively for integrated gradients.

Structural and computational challenges. Methods using input Hessians are limited by locality
and architectural constraints: they rely on fine-grained patch or token-level evaluations without a
natural mechanism for grouping, vanish in standard ReLU networks (Janizek et al., 2021), and incur
prohibitive computational costs that require specialized tricks to overcome (Pramanik et al., 2026).
Towards flexible, directional attributions. Our METAGAME framework resolves these limitations
by abstracting attributions over attributions. Acting as a universal wrapper for any first-order
method, it treats the underlying attribution as a new cooperative game. This natively preserves the
directional hierarchy (φj→i) and rigorously disentangles pure individual effects from interactions,
thereby avoiding the leakage of serial methods. Crucially, our methodology integrates with existing
theoretical advances: we demonstrate that meta-attributions are directional extensions of existing
set-based interactions, and METAGAME bridges local gradient insights with set-based perturbations.
The Metagame of Interpretability
We introduce meta-attributions φj→i, which can be read naturally as the influence of feature j on the

method ϕi (e.g., Meta-IG decomposes ϕIG
i ). To compute these, we conceptualize the METAGAME, a
cooperative game evaluating ϕi, and apply the Shapley value to extract interactions.

3.2
Directional Interactions via Meta-Attributions
To address the limitations of serial approaches, we introduce directional interactions for any arbitrary
first-order attribution method ϕi(f, x). The fundamental problem with serial applications, whether via
Shapley masking or integrated Hessians, is that both features xi and xj are jointly varied. This joint
variation inextricably entangles individual and pairwise effects. In practice, to cleanly separate these
components, we only care about the effect of changing xj given the value of xi. We therefore fix the
feature value xi and evaluate the marginal contribution of xj directly with respect to the first-order
attribution ϕi(f, x). To rigorously account for higher-order dependencies and fairly distribute the
interactions, we compute the Shapley value of feature j over the d −1 remaining features, treating ϕi
evaluated at the fixed xi as the underlying METAGAME.

3.3
Practical Considerations for Applying the METAGAME to Interpret Transformers
While our theoretical considerations focus on the foundational attribution methods, the general
concepts and intuitions behind METAGAME are also relevant to the modern interpretability approaches
for transformers. We effectively demonstrate this through three settings of increasing abstractness.
Meta-AttnLRP. AttnLRP (Achtibat et al., 2024) is a backpropagation-based, attention-aware method
to attribute single input tokens into transformer predictions. While it has shown state-of-the-art
faithfulness performance in language and vision tasks, it is unable to capture token-token synergies.
Meta-AttnLRP complements its interpretation with directional second-order interactions by comput-
ing a Shapley value of the exact METAGAME defined as: players := input tokens, game function :=
AttnLRP (cf. Definition 2). Specifically for language models, we simply aggregate token attributions
and interactions across the generated multi-token output sequence as detailed in Appendix C.2.

Meta-Grad-ECLIP. Grad-ECLIP (Zhao et al., 2024) is a gradient-based method to measure the
influence of image and text tokens on the embeddings of vision-language encoders. Similarly to
AttnLRP, it cannot quantify the synergy between the image and text modalities. Our proposed Meta-
Grad-ECLIP quantifies directional cross-modal interactions by computing Shapley values of text
tokens in the attribution of image tokens. METAGAME thus becomes: players := text tokens, game
function := Grad-ECLIP of image tokens (cf. Figure 2). Extending this idea, one can also compute a
bi-token Shapley interaction of the METAGAME, which can be treated as measuring synergy between
a token pair and the image, e.g. black-dog and hydr-ant in Figure 2. Finally, METAGAME could
also be implemented the other way around to quantify the second-order effects of image patches on
the attribution of text tokens, which we do not investigate in this work. As a minor contribution of
this paper, we extend the attention- and gradient-based methods originally proposed for CLIP to the
modern SigLIP-2 architecture (Tschannen et al., 2025) as detailed in Appendix C.3.
Meta-ConceptAttention. ConceptAttention is not a standard attribution method; it allows attributing
concepts (text tokens) to image patches generated by text-to-image diffusion transformers. However,
it is limited by producing highly context-dependent interpretations (cf. Helbling et al., 2025, Sec-
tion 5.5 & Figure 15). We aim to alleviate context dependence and interpret the second-order effects
of concepts. As the most abstract application of our framework, Meta-ConceptAttention computes a
Shapley value of the METAGAME defined as: players := concepts, game function := ConceptAttention.
Crucially, our implementation requires only a single model forward pass, just like ConceptAttention,
and computes marginal contributions using the cached model’s embeddings, making it viably efficient.
We provide further methodological and implementational details in Appendix C.4.
Scaling the computation. For small METAGAMES (d < 20), we compute the Shapley value exactly,
jointly obtaining φj→i for all targets i in one vectorized pass. For larger ones, we recommend SOTA
approximators from the shapiq library (Muschalik et al., 2024): Monte Carlo sampling amortizes
well with moderate d and many targets, while regression-based approximators (Witter et al., 2025)
better focus the budget when d is large but only specific targets matter, at the cost of one fit per target.
Applications and Experimental Results
Here, we demonstrate that the METAGAME offers useful insights: (i) when quantifying token interac-
tions in language models, (ii) when explaining similarity in vision-language encoders, and (iii) when
interpreting concepts in multimodal diffusion transformers. See Appendix D for details regarding the
experimental setup, implementations, and hyperparameters.

4.2
Explaining Similarity in Vision-Language Encoders
We demonstrate the broader applicability of METAGAME to improve three popular interpretabil-
ity methods: generic attention (Chefer et al., 2021), MaskCLIP (Dong et al., 2023), and Grad-
ECLIP (Zhao et al., 2024), which compute saliency maps for vision-language encoders. We bench-
mark the performance of these algorithms quantitatively using the pointing interaction recognition
metric proposed in (FIxLIP, Baniecki et al., 2025b), and experiment with three model architectures:
CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), SigLIP-2 (Tschannen et al., 2025), MetaCLIP-2 (Chuang et al., 2025).
Table 2 reports the state-of-the-art performance of meta-attributions in consistently improving the
interaction detection on ImageNet-1k across various models and methods, outperforming even the
second-order FIxLIP baseline in this task. Interestingly, our proposed Meta-MaskSigLIP approach
is the most faithful to interpret SigLIP-2, a result confirmed on two model patch sizes (cf. App.,
Table 4). Meta-Attention outperforms Meta-Grad-ECLIP on the overall largest, most recent, and
best-performing MetaCLIP-2 model (Table 2). Beyond faithfully attributing bi-token interactions,
computing Shapley interactions for the METAGAME of these attributions quantifies the tri-token
influence on the model’s prediction as illustrated across Appendix, Figures 8 & 9.
4.3
Interpreting Concepts in Multimodal Diffusion Transformers
Finally, we show the potential of METAGAME to improve the interpretability of text-to-image diffusion
transformers. Meta-ConceptAttention provides two valuable utilities in this task: the Shapley value of
ConceptAttention improves on CA’s main limitation-context-dependence, while the directional meta-
attribution explains the influence between pairs of concepts (see Figure 4). Following Helbling et al.
(2025), we benchmark Meta-ConceptAttention on a proxy segmentation performance of the FLUX.1
[schnell] text-to-image model (Black Forest Labs, 2024) on the Pascal VOC dataset (Everingham
et al., 2015), including single-object and multiclass images. We extend the evaluation to the more
challenging MS COCO benchmark (Lin et al., 2014). Contrary to the original experimental setup, we

Related Work
While METAGAME shares a naming affinity with the meta-evaluation problem (Hedström et al., 2023),
which quantifies attribution quality, our framework focuses on second-order interaction effects.
Backpropagation-based attribution. Several gradient-based methods capture higher-order dynam-
ics implicitly by aggregating attributions in a local neighborhood around the input, e.g. Smooth-
Grad (Smilkov et al., 2017; Zhou et al., 2025), expected gradients (Erion et al., 2021; Baniecki et al.,
2025a), or through model randomization tests (Adebayo et al., 2018; Binder et al., 2023). Sikdar
et al. (2021) utilizes integrated directional gradients to explicitly quantify group interactions, while
Eberle et al. (2022) extend layer-wise relevance propagation to quantify interactions in deep similarity
networks via second-order Taylor decomposition. Specifically in data attribution, Wang et al. (2025)
leverage gradient-Hessian-gradient products to capture interactions between training samples.

Removal-based attribution. Game-theoretic attribution is continuously evolving to capture complex
dependencies, from asymmetric Shapley values for causal priors (Frye et al., 2020), to n-Shapley
values recovering a functional decomposition of the model (Bordt and von Luxburg, 2023), and
unified frameworks for higher-order interactions (Fumagalli et al., 2025). Other advances include
discrete representations of mixed partial derivatives (Tsang et al., 2020) and mitigations for non-
convex games (Chang et al., 2025). However, METAGAME shifts the paradigm: rather than evaluating
the model output directly, it treats the first-order attribution itself as the value function of a cooperative
game to extract directional interactions. Finally, concurrent efforts by Kang et al. (2025) and Butler
et al. (2025) focus on scaling token interactions in large language models via Fourier expansions.
Applications. Despite ongoing skepticism regarding the specific properties of some feature attribution
methods (Bilodeau et al., 2024; Böhle et al., 2024), they remain crucial for analyzing large vision
models (Achtibat et al., 2023; Fel et al., 2023), e.g. in medical imaging (Chrabaszcz et al., 2025).
Komorowski et al. (2026) leveraged AttnLRP to guide decoding and improve instruction-following
in language models like Gemma 3. Explicit second-order methods, such as integrated Hessians,
successfully explain CLIP (Moeller et al., 2025) and LLMs (Pramanik et al., 2026). Similarly, Shapley
and Banzhaf interactions provide actionable insights in video-language training (Jin et al., 2024),
hyperparameter optimization (Wever et al., 2026), and survival analysis (Langbein et al., 2026).
Attention. The debate over whether attention faithfully explains transformers remains central,
particularly regarding how to aggregate these values into an explanation while accounting for other
network components (Wiegreffe and Pinter, 2019; Bibal et al., 2022). Growing evidence highlights its
limitations across vision (Komorowski et al., 2023), language (Achtibat et al., 2024), and multimodal
domains (Zhao et al., 2024). Nonetheless, attention remains a practical tool for locating concepts in
multimodal diffusion transformers (Helbling et al., 2025). To overcome the inherent limitations of
standard transformer architectures (Dziri et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2025), third-order attention between
input tokens is increasingly explored (Sanford et al., 2023; Kozachinskiy et al., 2025).
Conclusion
In this work, we introduced the METAGAME and meta-attributions as a mathematically principled in-
terpretability framework that complements existing methods for quantifying second-order interactions.
We demonstrated its versatility and impact across three areas of AI: analyzing token interactions in
instruction-tuned language models, explaining vision-language encoders, and interpreting concepts in
multimodal diffusion transformers. Ultimately, the foundational constructs behind METAGAME offer
a promising path toward more credible machine learning systems through future empirical research.
Limitations. Our theory is limited by assuming baseline masking/imputation-the most principled
approach to removing tokens/features from machine learning model inputs. Empirically, while it
is feasible to compute Shapley values exactly for games with d < 20, such as captions in vision-
language encoders and concepts in diffusion transformers, one must rely on efficient algorithms
to approximate them for larger games, such as language model prompts. We further acknowledge
that our language model analysis is an illustrative application of a few exemplary prompts; rigorous
method comparisons would also require faithfulness measurements (Achtibat et al., 2024), such as
token-pair insertion/deletion curves (Baniecki et al., 2025b). Finally, quantifying higher-order effects
inherently increases both computational cost and cognitive burden for the user.
Broader impact. We do not anticipate any negative societal impacts from this work, as it focuses on
foundational interpretability techniques intended to improve our understanding of transformers.

Illustrative Example
We aim to cross-compare complementary behaviors of various token attribution approaches on
a simple illustrative example. For that, we train a single-layer, single-head transformer with 760
parameters to solve integer addition in the form of a {+,-} b = y, where inputs a and b are integers
from [−9, 9]. The model uses RMSNorm, GELU, a learned absolute positional embedding, and
is trained without biases. It achieves a near-perfect mean squared error on both the in-distribution
validation set and a held-out test set comprising the unseen (a,b) pairs. For an intuitive case, we train
the model to recognize the masking token <unk> and regularize it to predict 0 for a fully masked input
sequence comprising three <unk> tokens (the so-called empty set giving baseline value). Ultimately,
the model predicts the expected output value over a distribution of input tokens in the <unk> position.
Our demo is easy to run on a CPU and fully explorable with the code attached to the paper. Figure 6
extends Figure 5 to show three representative input examples that build intuition for how Shapley
values and interactions behave, how they relate to gradient-based approximations, how AttnLRP’s
interpretation differs, and how our METAGAME approach decomposes the first-order attributions.

Extended Methods
C.1
Potential Extensions of the METAGAME and Meta-Attributions
While the METAGAME framework and the resulting meta-attributions are primarily formulated in the
context of token/feature attribution methods, their underlying principles are highly generalizable and
can be directly extended to a variety of other applications and settings.
Extension to other masking strategies. In our formulation, we evaluate the partially masked
functions f(S; x) and their corresponding first-order attributions ϕ(f, x) using standard baseline
imputation, e.g. replacing absent tokens with an <unk> or <mask> token. However, the METAGAME
framework is agnostic to the underlying perturbation scheme. Our approach can be extended to
employ other established removal techniques, such as feature marginalization over a background
dataset (Baniecki et al., 2025a) or conditional imputation (Sundararajan and Najmi, 2020).
Extension to higher-order interactions. Throughout this work, we compute directional meta-
attributions φj→i(f, x) by applying the standard Shapley value to the METAGAME, effectively
isolating the pairwise influence of feature j on the attribution of feature i. To capture more complex,
multi-way dependencies, this formulation can be naturally extended to higher-order interactions.
Instead of the first-order Shapley value, one could compute higher-order Shapley interaction in-
dices (Fumagalli et al., 2023) directly on the METAGAME, thereby quantifying how larger subsets of
features jointly influence the target attribution ϕi(f, x). We show such an example in Figure 2.
Extension to other applications such as global feature importance or data valuation. Motivated
by our three concrete applications, our directional meta-attributions are defined locally to explain a
model’s prediction for a specific input x. Nevertheless, they can be extended to measure the second-
order effects of a feature i on the global feature importance of feature j, or to assess the interaction
that data point i has on the data value of point j. By computing meta-attributions across the entire
data distribution, as SAGE (Covert et al., 2020) defines loss-based global feature importance, one can
construct a global meta-importance measure.

D.3
Interpreting Concepts in Multimodal Diffusion Transformers
We experiment with the FLUX.1 [schnell] model (Black Forest Labs, 2024, Apache License 2.0)
openly available on Hugging Face: black-forest-labs/FLUX.1-schnell. We loosely follow the
evaluation protocol proposed in (Helbling et al., 2025) to implement the zero-shot image segmentation
benchmark on three popular datasets:
• Pascal VOC (Everingham et al., 2015) validation set comprises 1,449 images: 927 with a single
annotated concept and 522 with 2-5 concepts. The label space contains 20 ‘thing’ classes.
• MS COCO (Lin et al., 2014, CC-BY-4.0) validation set comprises 5,000 panoptically annotated
images. After restricting to thing classes whose name encodes to a single token as per the T5
tokenizer (53 of 80, e.g. retaining cat, couch but excluding compound names like hot dog),
4,660 images remain eligible: 1,638 with one in-vocabulary thing class and 3,022 with 2-14.
• ImageNet-Seg (Guillaumin et al., 2014) provides 4,276 binary masks; we keep the 3,535 inputs
whose label maps to a single-T5-token concept (53 classes, disjoint from the MS COCO classes).
Each image contains a single foreground class (concept).
We use activations from the last 10 multimodal attention layers (ℓ∈{9, . . . , 18}), enable cross-
concept attention (joint mode), and feed an empty prompt to the text-to-image model. Crucially, five
literal background concepts are always-present context C: {background, floor, grass, tree, sky}. As
ablations, we additionally evaluate the last 5 layers only, disable cross-concept attention, and insert
an artificial prompt "a {concept1}, a {concept2}, . . ." as in the original ConceptAttention protocol.
We evaluate two tasks per dataset: single concept - binary foreground/background per (image,
concept) pair, restricted to images containing a single in-vocabulary concept; and multiple concepts -
per-pixel argmax across all in-vocabulary concepts plus the five background concepts. ImageNet-Seg
can be evaluated only on the single concept task.
In the description of metrics below, let yp denote the ground-truth category at pixel p (excluded if
not annotated), and C the set of in-vocabulary concepts, excluding the always-present background
concepts. Each method produces a per-concept saliency map sc ∈RH×W over image pixels; the
per-pixel prediction ˆyp is obtained by argmax over {sc}c∈C together with the five background-concept
channels on the multiple concepts task, or by min-max normalizing sc and thresholding at its mean
on the single concept task.
